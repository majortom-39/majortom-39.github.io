Currys: Conversions
on Demand

Funnel Diagnosis · Checkout & Payments · Buyer Research

Associate PM · Woxro client work Currys · UK electronics retailer 61K added conversions

Currys didn't need more traffic. It needed the traffic it already had to stop abandoning carts. My job on the engagement was the diagnosis: find where ready buyers were actually stalling, and make the case for which fixes came first.

01The brief I was handed

A major UK electronics retailer with plenty of demand and a leak somewhere between "I want this" and "payment complete." The ask that reached me wasn't "redesign the site." It was closer to a question: where exactly are we losing buyers, and what do we fix first? That's a diagnosis job before it's a design job, which is why it landed with the PM.

02My part

I owned the read on buyer behavior. Two kinds of evidence, forced to agree. The funnel data told me where people left: which step, at what rate, on which devices. The research told me why: watching buyers work through the flows and surveying shoppers about how they decide, hesitate, and abandon. Neither source alone earns a diagnosis. Data without the why produces redesigns that miss; interviews without the numbers produce anecdotes with confidence.

Three stall points kept showing up in both: product search that made buyers work too hard to find the right item, a checkout with more steps than intent survives, and a payment wall that turned people away when their preferred method wasn't offered. That last one was the sharpest finding, because a buyer at the payment step has already decided to buy. Losing them there isn't a persuasion problem; it's the store refusing money over an integration.

I made the sequencing call. With limited engagement time, everything couldn't be first. I ranked the fixes by measured drop-off rather than by what would look best in a portfolio: payment methods and checkout first, because that's where buyers with money in hand were leaving, then search, then the supporting product imagery.

I defined how we'd count it. Before any fix shipped, I pinned the measurement: conversions attributable end to end, not clicks, not sessions. If the number couldn't be traced, it didn't count.

The checkout, payment, and product-search flows, reworked in the order the measured drop-off told us to fix them.

03How we shipped it

The build was the studio team's craft: designers reworked the checkout and search flows, engineers wired the additional payment methods, and the product pages got clearer, enlarged imagery at the moment the purchase decision happens. My lane was keeping each change tied to the leak it was meant to plug, so we could tell afterward which fix earned what, and pushing back when a nice-to-have tried to jump the queue.

04What moved

The engagement's measured outcome:

61K
additional sales conversions, measured end to end
Checkout
simplified, with payment friction identified as the primary blocker
Search
streamlined so high-intent buyers reached the right product faster

The number belongs to the whole team that built the fixes. What I'll claim is the shape of it: the fixes that produced it were chosen and ordered by the diagnosis, and the diagnosis was mine.

05What it taught me

Conversion work is diagnosis work. The 61K didn't come from adding anything customers asked for; it came from removing what stopped them at the moments they'd already decided to buy. Fix the funnel before you feed it. Everything else is amplifying a leak.
