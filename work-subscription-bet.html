The Subscription Bet

01The signal in the cohorts

Booking frequency wasn't evenly distributed. Retention-cohort analysis surfaced a clear high-frequency segment booking well above the platform average, behavior that signals real willingness to pay for a better deal, not casual browsing. That cohort became the launch target, and its unit economics became the constraint every pricing decision had to satisfy.

The BBPro membership tiers and benefits, priced and structured through A/B testing on real retention data.

02Pricing tested, not guessed

The cohorts told us who; they couldn't tell us what they'd pay. Tier structure, discount depth, and framing went through price A/B tests before full rollout, finding the version that converted without eroding margin on bookings that would have happened anyway. The output was a membership with benefits deep enough to change booking behavior, priced so heavy usage stayed profitable.

The test design was deliberately narrow. A handful of tier-and-price variants went to matched slices of the target cohort, and each one was scored on two numbers, not one: conversion, and net margin per member. That second measure was the guardrail. A cheaper tier that converted better but pulled in the heaviest bookers could quietly lose money, because on this platform every member booking is a discounted cost, not accretive revenue. I modeled each variant as unit economics first (average discount depth times expected booking frequency against the membership fee) and only then looked at which one people actually chose. The winner was the structure that moved behavior while keeping the heaviest users above break-even.

03Launch, and what happened

BBPro went from discovery through rollout as a paid loyalty subscription. In its first year, 25% of the target segment converted, roughly 1,000 paying members, and that base became the platform's most valuable audience: the members later powering the Visa × Samsung partnership growth and the off-peak offers program.

The launch itself was targeted, not broadcast. The offer went to the cohort the data had nominated, in the surfaces where those users already booked, framed around the behavior they already had: you book this often, here is what that habit is worth as a membership. What I watched after launch was not just sign-ups but what members did next, because a loyalty subscription only works if the membership changes booking behavior instead of just taxing it. Members leaned into the benefit exactly the way the model needed them to, often enough to feel the value, not so hard that the economics broke.

And the trust details were product decisions, not legal boilerplate: clear eligibility, no auto-renew traps, refund terms a member could actually use. A loyalty product is a promise about the next twelve months, and every fine-print trick you resist is churn you don't have to win back later.

05What it taught me

The best acquisition channel is sometimes the users you already have, if you read the data closely enough to find them. A 25% conversion against a cohort you defined yourself is a different, more defensible number than 2% against everyone. And the discipline split matters: cohorts choose the audience, experiments choose the price. Mixing those two up is how subscriptions get mispriced.
