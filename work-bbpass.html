COVID emptied Dubai's tourism market, and the recovery came back lopsided, a roaring winter peak and a summer trough that stayed dead. The fix wasn't more discounting. It was discounting in the right place.

01Reading a broken market

Post-reopening, the data told a two-part story. Tourists returned seasonally: strong in the winter peak, gone in the brutal summer. But residents and expats never left, a durable, underserved audience whose staycation demand was price-elastic exactly when the market was emptiest. Dubai itself institutionalized this play: the city runs a summer-long festival of citywide promotions to stimulate off-season demand, so the market rhythm was proven, the question was whether membership offers could ride it.

The baseline problem was real: 2020–21 booking data was COVID-poisoned and useless for planning. We anchored on pre-COVID seasonality curves as the reference for what "normal" off-peak demand looked like, and measured recovery against that.

Seasonality-adaptive member offers, the levers I used to pull off-peak demand back up without touching peak-season margins.

02Discount the trough, protect the peak

Revenue-management logic drove the design. Peak demand is inelastic, discounting it burns margin on bookings that would happen anyway. Off-peak demand is elastic, and vendors sit on empty capacity whose marginal cost is near zero, which meant they would co-fund deeper member deals to fill it.

So member offers became adaptive: deeper discounts in off-peak months targeted at resident staycations, exclusive access instead of discounts at peak, with offer timing and depth set from the pre-COVID demand curves. The membership's trust infrastructure, clear eligibility, renewals without auto-renew traps, fair refund terms, kept the program worth committing to while the offer calendar changed around members.

The vendor conversation was the underrated half of the work. An empty summer slot at a waterpark or a dining venue has a marginal cost close to zero, so vendors had every reason to co-fund deeper member deals rather than let the capacity die. I ran those conversations vendor by vendor: which months hurt them most, what depth they could carry, and what we would fund from our side. The result was a calendar of offers that felt generous to members without either side quietly subsidizing bookings that would have happened anyway.

Measurement kept the same discipline. Every offer window was read against the pre-COVID curve for that same period, so the question was never "did bookings go up," it was "did bookings beat what this month normally does." That's the difference between a promo that shifts demand and one that just relabels it. The 2.2× is off-peak bookings against that seasonal baseline, which is the version of the number I can defend line by line.

04What it taught me

Discounts aren't a lever, they're a placement decision. The same discount that builds incremental demand in a trough burns pure margin at peak. And when a crisis destroys your baseline, the honest move is to say so and anchor on the last clean data, measuring recovery against a real reference beats celebrating growth over a crater.
