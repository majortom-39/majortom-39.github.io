01The context

Booking Bash grew up web-first. By the time the mobile bet was made, the ceiling was obvious: travel discovery and booking behavior had moved into apps, and the platform's highest-frequency users, the BBPro members and power bookers, had no home on the surface where they actually lived. The mandate wasn't "have an app." It was to move a working marketplace of 500+ venue listings onto two new platforms without breaking a booking flow that was earning revenue every day.

That last constraint set the tone for the whole project. A redesign can afford a rough first week; a checkout that processes real money cannot. So before a single screen was designed, I inventoried what the web product actually did, not what its documentation said it did. Years of client work had taught me that the gap between those two is where migrations die, and this one had years of accumulated behavior to account for.

02What I owned

The roadmap and the sequencing. Discovery mapped every booking-flow edge case the web product had accumulated over years, cancellation-policy tiers, multi-currency display, open-dated tickets, and re-specced them for mobile constraints: offline states, push-driven confirmations, and an in-app checkout and payment flow that had to feel faster than the web it replaced.

The pod. I ran a cross-functional engineering-design pod through agile delivery: sprint planning, spec reviews, and platform-parity triage. Date pickers, payment sheets, and deep links all behave differently across iOS and Android, and every difference is a support ticket waiting to happen. My rule for the pod was that parity bugs outranked new features until launch, which is an easy thing to say and a genuinely unpopular thing to enforce in sprint planning. We shipped parity first and earned the right to app-only features afterward.

The re-engagement loop. The app's real advantage over web wasn't the interface, it was permission to speak. Push re-engagement targeted high-frequency users with booking-relevant moments rather than noise. I treated notification design as offer design: a push had to carry something the user would thank us for, an off-peak member deal, a booking reminder, a confirmation, or it didn't ship. Burning notification permission on announcements is how apps get muted, and a muted app is a web tab with extra steps.

The native app experience we launched, with in-app checkout and push re-engagement that the mobile web could never do.

03An honest cohort

The success metric was scoped deliberately: bookings per user among migrated web users, people who had a web baseline to compare against. That cut avoids the classic mobile-launch trap of celebrating app-store installs while measuring nothing about behavior change. Against their own web baseline, migrated users booked 45% more.

What the cut deliberately excludes matters as much as what it includes. Brand-new users who arrived through the app stores have no baseline, so counting them would have inflated the number with people we couldn't attribute. And a per-user frequency metric can't be juiced by a marketing spike the way raw booking totals can. When I present 45%, I can explain exactly who is in the denominator, which is the whole point of choosing it.

05What it taught me

Platform migrations succeed on the boring edge cases nobody puts in the pitch deck. The 45% didn't come from a feature, it came from removing every reason a booking would fail to complete on the new surface, then using push to show up at the right moment instead of the loudest one.
