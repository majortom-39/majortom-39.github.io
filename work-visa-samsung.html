The Visa × Samsung

A subscription can only grow as fast as people discover a reason to join. Instead of paying to convince strangers one at a time, we put the membership inside brands people already trusted.

01The context

BBPro, Booking Bash's paid loyalty subscription, had proven its economics: a high-frequency member base built from a targeted power-user segment. The question was the next wave. Paid acquisition against cold audiences meant paying full CAC for users with unproven intent, and the math got worse with every incremental cohort.

The discovery insight: our best future members already carried a signal of trust and spending power in their wallets and pockets, Visa cardholders and Samsung device owners. The distribution question became a product question: could membership live inside those ecosystems as a benefit, instead of being advertised at them?

I spent real time on the arithmetic before anyone pitched a partner. A member is only profitable if the membership fee covers the discounts they will consume, so a channel that floods us with the wrong members is worse than no channel. That framing shaped everything downstream: we were not buying installs, we were selecting for people whose travel and spending behavior already looked like our best cohort. Partners were the filter, not just the megaphone.

02The bet

Distribution beats persuasion. We structured co-branded partnerships with Visa, Samsung, and ADCB, positioning BBPro as a cardholder benefit and a device-launch perk rather than another discount banner. Each partner brought a pre-qualified audience; we brought the booking inventory and the membership value.

The three deals were not the same product wearing three logos. The Visa arrangement was card-linked: eligible cardholders unlocked a complimentary year of the top membership tier, which made the benefit feel like part of the card, not a promotion stapled to it. The Samsung arrangement was device-linked, tied to a phone launch, so buyers of that phone found a year of membership waiting inside their new device. Same underlying membership, two completely different discovery moments, and each needed its own positioning, eligibility logic, and redemption journey. Treating them as one campaign would have flattened exactly the thing that made them work.

Co-branded launch creative, produced through the AIGC pipeline I built and trained the team on.

03What I owned

The GTM, end to end. Positioning per partner audience, the launch sequencing, and the offer architecture, which member benefits were exclusive to which channel, and how redemption flowed back into the marketplace. Sequencing mattered more than it sounds: each launch had to land inside the partner's own calendar, a card program cycle on one side, a device launch on the other, so I was effectively running our roadmap against two external roadmaps we didn't control.

Productizing eligibility. A partnership on a slide is easy; a partnership in production means eligibility rules the system can verify: who qualifies, through which partner, what happens at renewal, and what happens when someone qualifies through two doors at once. I wrote those rules with the engineers as actual product spec, not deal terms, because every case the system couldn't decide on its own became a support ticket with a partner's brand attached to it. That rules layer is what made the channel scale without manual exception handling.

The production problem nobody glamorizes. A co-brand launch demands campaign creative at a volume traditional production can't afford: every offer, in every format, versioned per partner and market. I built a GenAI (AIGC) production pipeline and then trained the creative team on it, so the workflow survived me being busy. It cut campaign production cost and time by 60%, which is what let us localize per partner without burning the launch budget. The training mattered as much as the pipeline; a tool only I could run would have been a bottleneck with better branding.

One honest note on the 3×: it counts the total active member base, including members whose first year came complimentary through a partner. That is exactly what the channel was designed to do, put the membership in the right hands at near-zero acquisition cost, and then let the product earn the renewal.

05What it taught me

Partnerships are a product decision wearing a business-development coat. The deal creates the audience; the eligibility rules, offer architecture, and renewal mechanics decide whether that audience converts and stays. And AI in production isn't a demo, it's margin: the team that learns the workflow keeps the savings long after the campaign ends.
